In a 9-minute race, which generation of Hong Kong boys would run the farthest?

Boys from 1999, who could become fathers by now, are likely to come out ahead. This indicates that today's boys may not match their parents' generation in terms of some physical fitness aspects.

Those from the past could run 1590 meters in nine minutes, whereas the physical fitness test results issued by the Education Bureau in 2015 indicate that boys nowadays would fall behind by more than 120 meters.

A similar situation was observed among girls, with a gap of about 22 meters.



This is not only about running speed and distance but also hints at a decline in the physical fitness and health of Hong Kong adolescents. The 9-minute run test, for example, reflects a drop in the relative peak oxygen uptake, an index of cardiovascular capacity.

In this running test, children and adolescents from Hong Kong found themselves in the 31% percentile when measured against global standards. This performance has led to a grade of D in "Physical Fitness" on the 2022 Hong Kong Report Card on Physical Activity for Children and Adolescents (Report Card), a report card issued to over 60 countries by the Active Healthy Kids Global Alliance since 2014.

In other aspects, such as tests measuring skinfolds in triceps and calf, the subcutaneous fat thickness in high school boys increased by 7.7%, while in girls, it rose by 12%.

Insufficient physical activity and prolonged sedentary time among our kids may explain this situation, said Hon Sze Sze, the Vice-Chairman of the Physical Fitness Association of Hong Kong.

While the World Health Organization (WHO) reports that over 80% of the world's adolescent population does not engage in enough physical activity in 2022, in Hong Kong, this kind of lifestyle is not a new thing among children and teenagers.

In a 2005-2006 survey by the Department of Health, less than 15.3% of Hong Kong children between 5 and 14 met the WHO's recommendations on physical activity for Health, which is 60 minutes of moderate- to vigorous-intensity physical activity (ideal physical activity level) daily. Also, 58.4% of respondents spent over two hours daily on screens such as TV, computers and video games.

The questions then arise: Has this situation worsened in the recent decade? Why is Hong Kong's younger population disengaged from physical activity? Could digital devices be contributing to the problem?

Continued Falling Physical Activity Level

According to the Report of Population Health Survey 2020-22, young people in Hong Kong aged from 15 to 24 engaged in vigorous physical activity for an average of 2.6 days per week, compared to the overall population average of 3.5 days. Even for moderate physical activity, the frequency for young people was 2.2 days, lower than the overall mean of 3.2 days.

For the duration of daily physical activity, the time young people spent exercising dropped from 103.7 minutes to 61.7 minutes compared to the figure in 2015, a reduction of nearly 70%.



When asked about the reasons for not doing exercise, almost half of young people responded that they were either too tired to do sports or too lazy to motivate their bodies. Another 33.6% of them said they had no time left for exercise.

Not in the mood for exercise, the reluctance to engage in exercise can also be observed if this age group is narrowed down to students, at most, only 26% of them met the WHO's ideal physical activity level, as indicated by a score of D- in "Overall Physical Activity on the 2022 Report Card.

In Asia, only Japan achieved a grade of B- or higher in 2022, indicating that up to 66% of children met the recommended level of physical activity.

The overall score for physical activity of all 57 participant countries was D in 2022. Not being in the mood for exercise can be a global issue.

I am not in The Mood for Exercise, so I Choose to Play My Phone

Waking up at 7 in the morning, the 18-year-old boy instinctively reached for his phone. He spent about 10 minutes scrolling through social media, checking for any updates he might have missed while sleeping. For him, this was a simple way to wake up his still-sleeping brain.

Cheng is a Form 5 student from an aided local secondary school at Tin Shui Wai. It was a typical school day, but according to his screen time data from Apple Health, he did not sleep until 2 at midnight. In the last week, Cheng's phone usage averaged 11.5 hours per day, with Instagram accounting for nearly 12 hours of his total screen time that week.

Cheng said, "Instagram is my go-to when I am bored and tired because I am too exhausted to think. Normally, I would not choose to play mobile games. I just scroll through social media and keep in touch with friends."

Cheng's fatigue was not limited to mobile gamesit also extended to physical activity. He said that the exhaustion from school left him with no routine for exercise.

"I am not stressed by homework, but I am involved in a STEM project at school, which makes my daily routine quite busy," Cheng explained. "By the weekend, I usually sleep until 3 or 4 in the afternoon to make up for lost sleep during the week. I might go out with friends or kill time using phones, but generally, I do not choose to do any physical activity."

As for Tsz Ying Choi, a Form 2 student, a once-a-month ice skating session is enough. "I am not a fan of going out, so I usually sit at my desk on rest days, watching YouTube on my tablet or doing homework." Her average daily screen was 5 hours, and she acknowledged that digital devices indeed have, to some extent, influenced her level of physical activity.

Eelyn Wong, who is in Form 5 and takes the Physical Education elective subject, observed that some of her peers have become addicted to phones or video games, leading them to gradually quit exercising. She noted that this phenomenon is becoming increasingly common and is affecting younger age groups.

When leisure activities gradually converge to static, screen-based options, the choice of "get moving" is often pushed further and further back.

Digital device is the main challenge for getting young people moving. Things only got worse after smartphones came out. As children increasingly tether themselves to their smartphones, it is becoming challenging to engage them in physical activities, said Hon.

Hon is also a lecturer teaching the Physical Education and Recreation Management program at Hong Kong Baptist University. She noticed that whether during active practising sessions or static lectures, students are constantly clutching their phones. How to capture their attention and engage them without having to compete with their digital devices is a challenge for her.

She believed that this reliance on digital devices among young people originates from their early childhood. For many parents nowadays, their parenting is to give children an "electronic pacifier," such as tablets, TVs, and other electronic devices.

Although this approach frees up parents' time to focus on other tasks, this early exposure to digital devices fosters a strong dependency on children, leading to a lifestyle where sitting in front of a screen becomes normal.

As digital natives, only around of Hong Kong's primary and secondary students have followed the screen time guidelines, which is no more than 2 hours of recreational screen time per day, according to a score of D in Sedentary Behaviour on the 2022 Report Card. It deteriorated from a C in 2016.

Globally, the overall score in this aspect for all participating countries improved slightly from D in 2016 to D+ in 2022.

This lifestyle has been exposing the younger generation to an increased risk of non-communicable diseases such as cardiovascular diseases and diabetes, according to the WHO.

Then, the COVID-19 pandemic made this risk higher.

Students' physical fitness data, such as a 9-minute run, was not collected completely during the COVID-19 pandemic. Hon estimated that the outcomes might have deteriorated further than those in 2015, due to restrictions on outdoor physical movement and prolonged daily online lessons.

According to a study conducted by various universities in Hong Kong, before the pandemic, secondary school students were sedentary for an average of 10.79 hours per day. This figure increased by about an hour to 11.61 hours during school closures.

The overall daily screen time also saw a significant increase, from 8.09 hours to 10.64 hours. Concurrently, the time spent on exercises per day dropped by almost half, from 0.32 hours to 0.17 hours.

The Education Bureau's Data then showed that in 2021, just one year after the start of the COVID pandemic, the overweight rate among Hong Kong middle school students reached 26.8%, a considerable increase compared to 21.5% in 2015 and 17.41% in 2010.

The Active Healthy Kids Global Alliance highlighted that new habits have been formed by children and teenagers around the globe in response to the screen-centric indoor living new normal.

Hon remarked, "Once these habits were formed, it's uncertain whether they can be easily changed.

Do Schools Contribute to Increased Physical Activity, or Is The Reality More Complex?



When Hong Kong students lag behind the global average in physical activity level and physical fitness, Hong Kong schools were doing well in organizing student sports and providing sports resources.

Hong Kong got a B- in Organized Sport and Physical Activity in 2022 and progressed a successive two times from a C- in 2016, meaning that 60.1% of local secondary school students engaged in organised sport for at least one day per week. In this area, Hong Kong is catching up with Japan.

In addition, 67% to 73% of students in Hong Kong have benefited from enough sports facilities and resources for physical education (PE) provided in schools, as implied by a score of B in the School aspect.

Not only schools have made an effort, but in the past decades, the government has also carried out various sports promotion programs at the school level, for example, the School Sports Programme since 2001.

By launching different sports training and education projects and organising inter-school competitions, this programme aims to nurture students' interest in sports, boost their physical activity levels, improve their sports performance and identify potential student athletes, as outlined on its website.

The community, including initiatives like the Jockey Club's SportExplore Project, is paying attention to advocating for sports promotion at both the society and school levels.

After the collaborative efforts across various levels of schools, government, and society with their sports promotional programs, the question remains: Why has students' physical fitness continued to decline?

Hon believes that with students lacking motivation for sports, having physical education classes only once a week is insufficient for both increasing their activity levels and nurturing their interest in sports.

The least Physical Education Curriculum and Assessment Guide updated in 2023 mandates that general PE should constitute at least 5% of total lesson time in both primary and secondary education curriculums.

Given that total lesson time amounts to approximately 2,500 hours across three years of senior secondary education, the expected PE teaching time should be around 125 hours.

This figure in Japan stands at 262.5 hours, more than double that of Hong Kong. Its allocation of PE lessons is on par with that for Science Education and Social Education according to the Ministry of Education, Culture, Sport, Science and Technology of Japan.

A study conducted in 2019 across 10 local secondary schools reported that the PE teaching time varied from 80 to 100 minutes in a week or cycle. While this exceeded the minimum threshold, it still fell behind the recommended PE duration observed in other regions, such as the United States.

If the time allotted for physical education does not indicate the problem, the quality of PE can be another important index for assessing school PE effectiveness.

Although schools are expected to meet the minimum requirement for PE, the reality is that not many schools have recorded active policies, including daily PE, recess, and outdoor time, as highlighted by a study interpreting the results of Hong Kong's 2022 Report Card.

This study also mentioned that among 26 local schools, only 18.5% of PE class time was spent on activities that require students to engage in exercises.

Regarding this situation, Cheng felt that the teachers are primarily focused on getting students to participate, without placing much emphasis on the quality of PE classes.

Cheng has one 75-minute PE class in his weekly timetable. His Apple Watch recorded his health and activity metrics-everything from the distance he walks and runs to his active energy expenditure and exercise minutes.

His data of a typical Friday revealed that his active energy expenditure during his PE class was under 120 kilocalories, whereas that figure during the lunch break was 106 kilocalories.

To me, PE lessons are not a burden since teachers focus more on techniques teaching and our participation than physical exertion, Cheng said.

They would not push us too hard to pursue higher learning outcomes because they do not want to hinder our interest and motivation.

Prioritizing interest building over physical training was observed by the other two girls in their schools.

Wong mentions that the intensity of PE classes was only moderate, as the teacher often interrupted the exercises to provide explanations, leading to frequent breaks in physical activity.

However, Cheng, Wong, and Choi all highlighted the diversity in PE teaching content, mentioning that the curriculum often includes outdoor runs, dancing, jump rope, and ball games, depending on the teachers' plans.

But can this teaching style spark an interest in sports among students? The answer depends on the individual.

Wong, a PE elective student with an established exercise routine, thought that a single 80-minute PE class per week is unlikely to inspire those without regular exercise habits to develop an interest in physical activity.

Because they even showed unwillingness to exercise in PE class, it is unrealistic to expect them to do sports during their free time, said Eelyn. On the other hand, for those who enjoy sports, PE classes offer a great opportunity to explore various forms of exercise.

Though physical education classes in some schools may not help students achieve the ideal physical activity level and foster interest in sports, Cheng and Choi believed that one PE class per week is sufficient.

Cheng worried increasing the number of PE lessons on the schedule would disrupt other subjects' teaching plans, yet he was not concerned about any potential health risks from inefficient physical activity.

In fact, Cheng believed that he was not missing out on exercise.

He walks to school and is constantly on the move during breaks, running errands for different stuff. His classroom is on the fifth floor, so he has to walk up and down around ten flights of stairs while he had lunch outside the school, which is why his active energy expenditure peaked during his lunch break.

On school days, his step count often reaches around 13,000 steps, and he considers this a significant amount of physical activity.

owever, was the amount of physical activity he found sufficient really adequate?

The concept of "exercise minutes" recorded by an Apple Watch counts every full minute of movement at or above the intensity of a brisk walk and may serve as a rough standard of evaluation of the ideal physical activity level since brisk walking is generally considered a moderate-intensity exercise.

Cyril's exercise minutes did not reach the ideal physical activity level both on the day he did not have PE class and the day included PE. His exercise minutes during PE class were under 30 minutes, which accounted for less than half of the duration of PE class.

Aside from PE classes and breaks, most of the time at school, he was sedentary. His standing minutes were only xx% of time throughout the school time.

The same sedentary pattern was also suggested by Choi's data.

During a four-hour morning class, Choi was on her feet for only eight minutes. It was only during the following lunch break and PE class that her standing time increased to an average of 36 minutes per hour.

She recalled that she rather do her stuff seated over walking around or doing sports at the playground during breaks.

For Wong, who has an established exercise routine, her average standing time was about 10 minutes per hour, excluding the PE class. This was because she often played basketball with classmates on the school playground during breaks.

I think at least two classes are needed to encourage students to be more active, build exercise habits, and avoid prolonged sitting, said Wong.

This sit-oriented study mode promotes a kind of habitual sedentary behaviour, said Hon. In some Western countries, they have already tried to adopt a "sit and move" mode by using sit-stand desks to reduce students' sedentary time as well as increase their physical activity level.

A study published in 2022 has studied the benefits of this sit and move intervention for Hong Kong's primary school children. The result shows that this strategy can help children achieve a healthier lifestyle at multiple levels, such as improved physical activity level, physical literacy, sleep quality and executive functioning.

Hon believed that creating an active environment for students is beneficial, but schools are more grounded in reality when it comes to changing learning modes. This approach may not be feasible within Hong Kong's cultural context.

However, some private international schools in Hong Kong have implemented better active policies. Yet these schools tend to have higher tuition fees and stricter admission requirements compared to ordinary secondary schools.

Jill Xu, a young mother, has a 9-year-old son attending an international school in Lam Tin.

She chose this school because it offers a sports session every day after lunch and a mandatory swimming class once a week. She believes that this emphasis on PE will instil confidence, health, and better interpersonal communication skills in her son.

"Compared to the costs of his extracurricular classes each year, the nearly HK$200,000 for annual tuition fees seem minor."

Jill put her son in both squash and basketball classes taught by private facilities once a week.

Hon holded that the gap in physical activity degrees between children who are from different levels of socioeconomic status (SES) will extend. Economic resources can offer children more opportunities in advanced PE. Besides money, the awareness and perception of PE differ across families with varying SES.

The Underlying Reasons of Physical Inactivity Stem from PE in Early Childhood



Hong Kong's public sports facilities are not particularly scarce despite some shortcomings. However, as Hon notes, the challenge lies in encouraging people who do not usually exercise to make full use of these facilities.

The primary issue is the lack of motivation and interest, which Hon believes is connected to the PE received during early childhood to some extent.

In terms of parenting, beyond the use of electronic babysitting, today's parents may not be that physically fit themselves, limiting their ability to set a positive example for their children regarding physical activity.

According to the Report of Population Health Survey 2020-22, besides the younger generation, physical activity among people aged between 25 and 54 has also seen a sharp decline over the past decade.

In addition to education at home, environments in kindergarten may not be ideally suited for encouraging children's physical activity.

Hon explains that kindergartens in Hong Kong are often small, providing limited space for free play, which is crucial for developing big muscle groups in children. These muscles are essential for gross motor skills, which coordinate activities such as walking, running and throwing.

Gross motor skills are the basis for children's future sports performance, for example, coordination, reaction speed, balance and strength. Missing out on this development during early childhood makes it difficult to catch up later.

As a result, some children exhibit poor performance in sports, leading to a lack of interest and confidence in physical activity.

Hon believes that "when you excel at something, you tend to develop an interest in it. Conversely, repeated failures can suppress your interest."

As required in the Operation Manual for Pre-Primary Institutions 2023 issued by the Education Bureau, the minimum indoor activity area of institutions attended by children aged from 2 to 6 is 1.8 square metres, while an outdoor play area is not provided by every kindergarten.

If outdoor facilities are not available, the area of indoor play area should be at least 50% of the total classroom space.

This figure is not specified in Japan, but in Singapore, it is defined in the Early Childhood Development Agency's guidelines. The requirement for space for gross motor development activities should be 1/6 of the centre's capacity at 4m per child or 40m, whichever is greater.

A 2015 Legislative Council document titled "Consultancy Study on Accommodation Requirements for Kindergartens (LC Paper No. CB(4)1266/14-15(03))" once recommended that the ideal indoor activity area for kindergartens should be between 2.16 and 2.52 square meters per child, noting that the majority of kindergarten staff believed the space available for physical movement among students, whether indoors or outdoors, was inadequate.

Aside from limited space, Hon also believes that Hong Kong parents tend to be conservative, which could lead to misconceptions about free play, viewing it as lacking control. As with the "sit and move" classroom approach, this can result in poor feasibility.

When asked for suggestions to improve Hong Kong children's activity levels, Hon believes the key lies in cutting down on screen time and sedentary time. (new generation)

Since 2014, Hon has served as a convener and project advisor respectively for Jocky Club Keep-Fit Formula for Children (Keep-Fit Formula) and Jocky Club Home-School Physical Fitness Academy for Kindergarteners (Physical Fitness Academy), which are children's fitness promotion projects.

These programs aim to create a supportive environment for physical fitness development at both home and school by strengthening collaboration between families and schools, improving the professional skills of educators in physical fitness, and raising parents' awareness of the importance of physical fitness.

In the Keep-Fit Formula between 2015 and 2018, a total of 288 physical fitness lesson plans for kindergartens and primary schools were designed to help integrate physical activity with other subjects such as language classes and math, thereby increasing physical activity in the classroom.

The Physical Fitness Academy from 2019 to this year focused on teaching parents how to use small items, such as sponge balls, to engage in parent-child activities, aiming to increase children's physical activity and foster interest in it.

Hon said Various sectors of society are making efforts to address the issue of physical inactivity among our younger generation. The root cause lies in the lifestyle changes brought by screen time, with sedentary behaviour becoming the norm both at home and in schools.

She believes that these two factors are currently the most pressing areas that need to change.
